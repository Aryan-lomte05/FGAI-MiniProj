CBT FOR ANXIETY — PSYCHOEDUCATION GUIDE
Source: Adapted from WHO mental health gap action programme & NIMHANS resources

WHAT IS ANXIETY?
Anxiety is a natural human emotion — a warning signal your brain sends to prepare you for perceived threats. In moderate amounts, it improves performance. However, when anxiety becomes persistent, disproportionate, or interferes with daily life, it may indicate an anxiety disorder.

Common physical symptoms: racing heart, shallow breathing, muscle tension, sweating, dizziness, nausea.
Common cognitive symptoms: catastrophic thinking, mind going blank, difficulty concentrating, constant worry.
Common behavioural symptoms: avoidance, procrastination, reassurance-seeking, withdrawal.

THE CBT MODEL OF ANXIETY
Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) proposes that thoughts, feelings, and behaviours are interconnected. A triggering situation leads to an automatic thought ("I will fail this exam"), which produces an emotional response (anxiety), which then drives behaviour (avoidance). CBT works by identifying and challenging unhelpful thought patterns.

COMMON COGNITIVE DISTORTIONS IN ANXIETY:
1. Catastrophising — expecting the worst possible outcome ("If I fail this test, my life is over")
2. All-or-nothing thinking — seeing things in extremes ("Either I do it perfectly or it's a failure")
3. Mind reading — assuming you know what others think ("Everyone thinks I'm stupid")
4. Fortune telling — predicting negative futures without evidence
5. Emotional reasoning — believing something is true because it feels true ("I feel anxious, so something must be wrong")

CBT TECHNIQUES FOR MANAGING ANXIETY:

1. THOUGHT CHALLENGING (Cognitive Restructuring)
Step 1: Identify the anxious thought (write it down)
Step 2: Ask — what is the evidence FOR this thought? What is the evidence AGAINST?
Step 3: What would I say to a friend who had this thought?
Step 4: What is a more balanced, realistic way to think about this?

2. BREATHING EXERCISE — 4-7-8 TECHNIQUE
- Inhale slowly through nose for 4 counts
- Hold breath for 7 counts
- Exhale completely through mouth for 8 counts
- Repeat 4 cycles
- This activates the parasympathetic nervous system and reduces the stress response

3. GROUNDING — 5-4-3-2-1 TECHNIQUE
When overwhelmed, bring yourself back to the present moment:
- 5 things you can SEE right now
- 4 things you can TOUCH and feel
- 3 things you can HEAR
- 2 things you can SMELL
- 1 thing you can TASTE

4. WORRY TIME
Schedule a specific 20-minute period daily as "worry time." When anxious thoughts arise outside this window, write them down and defer them. This prevents anxiety from spreading through the entire day.

5. PROGRESSIVE MUSCLE RELAXATION
Tense each muscle group for 5 seconds, then release for 30 seconds. Start from feet, move upward. Notice the difference between tension and relaxation.

WHEN TO SEEK PROFESSIONAL HELP:
If anxiety persists for more than 2 weeks, significantly impairs academic or social functioning, or leads to panic attacks, please reach out to a counsellor, psychologist, or psychiatrist.
