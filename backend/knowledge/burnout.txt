BURNOUT IN STUDENTS — UNDERSTANDING AND RECOVERY
Source: Adapted from WHO, NIMHANS, and iCall (TISS) student mental health resources

WHAT IS BURNOUT?
Burnout is a state of physical, emotional, and mental exhaustion caused by prolonged, unrelenting stress. Unlike ordinary tiredness, burnout does not resolve with a single good night of sleep. It builds gradually and affects your ability to function across all areas of life.

Burnout was originally studied in workplace contexts but is now widely recognised in students — particularly engineering students in India who face high-pressure academic environments, placement anxiety, and social expectations simultaneously.

THE THREE DIMENSIONS OF BURNOUT (Maslach Framework):
1. EXHAUSTION — Feeling chronically drained. No energy even for things you used to enjoy. Physical fatigue persisting despite rest.
2. CYNICISM / DEPERSONALISATION — Emotional detachment. Starting to feel that college, studies, or your goals are meaningless. "What's the point?"
3. REDUCED EFFICACY — Doubting your ability to perform. Feeling like you are falling behind no matter what you do. Loss of confidence.

If you recognise all three, you may be experiencing burnout — not laziness, not failure, not weakness.

CAUSES COMMON IN INDIAN ENGINEERING STUDENTS:
- Continuous academic pressure with no recovery periods
- Placement anxiety and competitive comparison with peers
- Financial stressors and family expectations
- Social isolation, especially in hostels or when far from home
- Loss of hobbies and activities that previously gave pleasure
- Sleep disruption due to study schedules and screen time
- Identity questions — "Is this even what I want?"

HOW BURNOUT DIFFERS FROM DEPRESSION:
Burnout is primarily situational and context-specific. Someone with burnout may feel fine on a holiday but dread returning. Depression tends to be more pervasive — affecting mood, self-worth, and pleasure across all contexts. However, prolonged burnout can develop into clinical depression. Both are real and both deserve support.

RECOVERY STRATEGIES:
1. NAME IT — Acknowledging burnout is the first step. It is not a personal failure.
2. MICRO-RECOVERY — You cannot rest all at once. Build in small daily recovery periods: 20-minute walks, music, non-academic reading.
3. REDUCE COGNITIVE LOAD — Use lists, time-blocking, and "done lists" to reduce the mental overhead of open-ended tasks.
4. RECONNECT WITH INTRINSIC MOTIVATION — What did you care about before the pressure started? Even small reconnections matter.
5. SLEEP HYGIENE — Target minimum 7 hours. Consistent sleep and wake times reset your circadian rhythm and dramatically affect mood.
6. SOCIAL MICRO-DOSES — You do not need a social life. You need one or two genuine conversations per week. Even a 10-minute honest conversation reduces isolation.
7. SEEK SUPPORT — Talk to a counsellor. iCall (9152987821) offers free, confidential sessions with trained counsellors. TISS-backed, culturally grounded.

WHAT NOT TO DO IN BURNOUT:
- Do not try to "push through" without recovery — this deepens exhaustion
- Do not compare your pace to peers — their visible output does not reflect their internal state
- Do not use social media as rest — passive scrolling increases cortisol

ACADEMIC CONTEXT — SPEAKING TO FACULTY:
Many Indian colleges have provisions for medical or psychological leave. If burnout is affecting attendance or performance, document it. A counsellor's letter can sometimes be used to request extensions or accommodations. You have more options than you think.
