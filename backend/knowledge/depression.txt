DEPRESSION — UNDERSTANDING, RECOGNITION, AND SUPPORT
Source: WHO Mental Health Gap Action Programme (mhGAP), NIMHANS, iCall TISS

WHAT IS DEPRESSION?
Depression is not sadness. Sadness is a normal human emotion that passes. Depression is a clinical condition characterised by persistent low mood, loss of pleasure, and a range of cognitive and physical symptoms that significantly impair daily functioning.

Depression is one of the leading causes of disability worldwide. In India, it is severely underdiagnosed due to stigma, lack of awareness, and limited access to care. The lifetime prevalence in India is estimated at 5-7%.

Depression is not a character flaw. It is not weakness. It is not something you can "snap out of" through willpower. It has neurological, psychological, and social roots.

SYMPTOMS (2 weeks or more, most days):
CORE SYMPTOMS (at least one required for clinical diagnosis):
- Persistent low mood, tearfulness, emptiness
- Loss of interest or pleasure in activities previously enjoyed (anhedonia)

ADDITIONAL SYMPTOMS:
- Fatigue or loss of energy nearly every day
- Changes in appetite (significant weight loss or gain)
- Sleep disturbances (insomnia or sleeping too much)
- Feelings of worthlessness or excessive guilt
- Difficulty concentrating, thinking, or making decisions
- Psychomotor changes (moving or speaking more slowly than usual, or feeling agitated)
- Recurrent thoughts of death or suicidal ideation

If you are experiencing 5 or more of these for 2+ weeks, please speak with a mental health professional.

DEPRESSION IN COLLEGE STUDENTS — SPECIFIC PATTERNS:
- Often masked as "laziness" — difficulty getting out of bed, missing classes, not completing assignments
- Social withdrawal — stopping replies to messages, eating alone, avoiding hostel common areas
- Academic decline — difficulty concentrating, forgetting material, performance drop
- Loss of sense of future — "What's the point of studying" — not philosophical, but motivated by hopelessness
- Increased irritability (especially in men) instead of visible sadness
- Physical complaints without medical cause — headaches, stomach aches, chronic fatigue

WHAT HELPS (Evidence-Based):
1. PSYCHOTHERAPY — CBT and Interpersonal Therapy (IPT) are the gold-standard first-line treatments for mild-to-moderate depression. Both are available through iCall (TISS) for students at low/no cost.
2. MEDICATION — For moderate-to-severe depression, antidepressants (SSRIs) prescribed by a psychiatrist can be highly effective. Medication is not a sign of failure — it is a medical treatment for a medical condition.
3. EXERCISE — 30 minutes of aerobic exercise 3x per week has been shown to be as effective as medication for mild depression. This is not motivational advice — it is a documented physiological mechanism involving BDNF and neurotransmitter regulation.
4. SOCIAL CONNECTION — Even one high-quality social interaction per week has measurable antidepressant effects.
5. SLEEP REGULARISATION — Depression disrupts sleep architecture. Maintaining consistent sleep/wake times is a direct intervention.
6. BEHAVIOURAL ACTIVATION — See grounding document. Acting before feeling, not waiting to feel motivated.

WHAT DOES NOT HELP:
- Telling someone to "cheer up" or "think positive"
- Staying isolated waiting to "feel better first"
- Pushing through without any support or intervention

SAFE MESSAGING — HOW TO SUPPORT A FRIEND:
If someone you know is showing signs of depression:
- Listen without fixing. "That sounds really hard" is more powerful than advice.
- Ask directly: "Are you having thoughts of hurting yourself?" — directly asking does not plant the idea; it opens the door.
- Offer specific help ("I'll walk with you to the counselling centre") rather than generic ("Let me know if you need anything")
- Do not promise confidentiality if they disclose serious risk
